Graduate Research in High Speed TCP Protocols

Upon beginning my graduate studies I quickly discovered a passion for systems and networks topics. After taking several related courses I became interested in the effect of router buffer sizes on high-speed network protocols, and worked with Prof. Don Smith to design a study to examine this topic for my master's research project.

I conducted an exhaustive experimental and analytical study of the effect of reduced router buffer sizes on high-speed network protocols. Using TCL and C++, I ran simulations to validate the work of others. I was able to show conclusive proof of the detrimental impact of reduced router buffer size on performance, and make suggestions for improvements.

Graduate Teaching Assistant

I deeply enjoy helping others learn, especially beginners. For five semesters, I served as a TA in introductory courses for Java, Javascript/HTML, Matlab, and computer networks. During this time, I conducted lessons and tutoring sessions, held weekly office hours and one-on-one help sessions, and graded assignments for more than 200 students.

In my first semester as a TA, I was honored with a Student Undergraduate Teaching Award. Later on, I collaborated with instructors and other TAs for introductory courses to create more hands-on, relatable teaching methods for non-computer science students. Our efforts led to greater class participation, expansion of introductory courses, and higher pass rates.
